AI Generates Antibodies Beyond Human Imagination

LabGenius, led by founder and CEO James Field, is utilizing artificial intelligence (AI) to revolutionize the engineering of medical antibodies at its facility in South London. Unlike the slow and intricate process conducted by human protein designers, LabGenius employs machine learning algorithms, DNA sequencing machines, and automated robotic systems to largely automate the antibody discovery process.

Antibodies, the body's response to disease, are synthetic in pharmaceuticals to treat conditions like cancer. The traditional process involves sifting through millions of potential amino acid combinations, making experimental adjustments, and hoping for improved characteristics without unintended consequences.

LabGenius's approach begins with human scientists identifying a search space for potential antibodies. A machine learning model, with minimal human input, explores this space, designing over 700 initial options from a pool of 100,000 potential antibodies. The process involves automated testing, growth of antibodies based on genetic sequences, and biological assays on diseased tissue samples.

Experimental results are fed back into the machine learning model, refining its understanding of the antibody design space. Unlike conventional methods, LabGenius's approach avoids the pitfalls of endless minor tweaks that may compromise overall effectiveness.

The automated process takes just six weeks from problem setup to the completion of the first antibody batch, with unexpected and counterintuitive solutions emerging more quickly than traditional methods. LabGenius, having raised $28 million in funding, is exploring partnerships with pharmaceutical companies and envisions extending the automated approach to other forms of drug discovery.

James Field emphasizes that this AI-powered recipe for better care results in more effective antibodies with fewer side effects than those designed by humans. The approach identifies distinct and often counterintuitive molecules that may lead to better patient outcomes.
