Company plans to tackle battery affecting the environment

The CEO of European battery manufacturer Northvolt, Peter Carlsson, acknowledges the environmental impact of electric vehicle (EV) battery production and outlines the company's plan to significantly reduce emissions. According to Carlsson, the current production process emits over 100 kg of CO2 per kilowatt-hour of energy provided across the battery's lifetime, and Northvolt aims to decrease it to around 10 kg by 2030.

Northvolt's strategy focuses on three key elements: locating manufacturing in areas with abundant green energy, developing low-carbon or carbon-free supply chains, and incorporating a high degree of recycling. Carlsson emphasizes the importance of controlling a significant portion of the supply chain and regionalizing battery production to reduce carbon footprints and ensure a strategic supply.

The company collaborates with Galp in Portugal for lithium hydroxide refining, utilizing offshore wind and hydropower to establish a low-carbon lithium hydroxide processing plant. Carlsson addresses the need for regionalization, citing energy independence and practical industry requirements for just-in-time setups, even though China currently dominates the battery sector.

When asked about competition with China, Carlsson stresses the importance of scale in challenging incumbents. He believes that vertical integration, infrastructure placement in areas with low-cost renewable energy, and achieving scale can offset lower labor costs in Asia.

While EVs currently drive the majority of battery demand, Carlsson anticipates a rapid evolution in energy storage and grid storage. He mentions the growing need for energy storage to manage grid bottlenecks and accommodate increased renewable energy production, as well as the electrification of various industrial sectors.

Regarding future battery technologies, Carlsson sees continuous evolution in core battery technologies like lithium nickel manganese cobalt oxides and lithium iron phosphate. He highlights the emerging opportunity for sodium-ion batteries, which offer good thermal capabilities, excellent cyclability, and the use of alternative materials for sustainability.
