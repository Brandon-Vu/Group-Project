iPhone 12 Faces French Ban Due to Radiation Test Failure

Last week, France's regulatory body, the ANFR, banned Apple's iPhone 12 due to excessive radiation emissions. The ban has sparked questions about the timing, thoroughness of testing, potential dangers, and the nature of the emitted radiation.

All phones emit radio frequency (RF) radiation, including iPhones. RF is a type of electromagnetic radiation with frequencies between 20 kHz and 300 GHz. The ANFR tested the iPhone 12's RF emissions in "limb" and "trunk" scenarios, where it failed the 0-mm limb test but passed the 5-mm trunk test.

Testing involves measuring Specific Absorption Rate (SAR), indicating energy transferred to tissue. The iPhone 12's 0-mm limb test exceeded the 4 W/kg limit, raising concerns about potential heating effects. The ANFR's database lists results for various phones, including those that failed, such as Motorola Edge and Samsung models.

Apple responded with plans for a software update to comply with French protocols. Other components contributing to RF radiation in the iPhone 12 include the Qualcomm X55 5G modem, Wi-Fi/Bluetooth chip, wireless charging tech, and U1 ultra-wideband chip. The ANFR tests are more demanding, employing a 0-mm distance, unlike the standard 5 mm in the US.

Phones' RF emissions vary due to factors like signal strength, RF frontend chips, 5G modem, and software. The Samsung Galaxy S20 Ultra, sharing components with the iPhone 12, emitted significantly less RF radiation in ANFR tests. France's testing standards are among the strictest globally.

RF radiation from phones falls within the non-ionizing electromagnetic spectrum, considered less dangerous than ionizing radiation. Studies on mobile phone use and health effects remain inconclusive, with conflicting findings and outdated research contributing to the uncertainty.

In conclusion, the iPhone 12's failure in France's radiation tests has raised concerns about the potential health impact of RF radiation. Apple's commitment to addressing the issue through a software update reflects the evolving nature of regulations and standards in the mobile phone industry.
